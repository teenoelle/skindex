> i want to build an app or site using a similar sheet     but on google drive that lets people add products to     identify potential skin irritants. what ideas do you     have?                                                  
● Here are some ideas across different directions:

  ---                                                      Core Concept Options
                                                           1. Public ingredient scanner (input → instant results)
  User pastes an ingredients list (from a product label
  or INCIDecoder), and the app instantly flags known
  irritants from a curated database. No account needed.
  Simple and high-utility.

  2. Personal product tracker (like the sheet, but
  multi-user)
  Each user has their own list of products they've tried,
   rated, and tracked. They can log reactions, mark
  status, and build a personal "safe list" over time.

  3. Community-sourced database
  Users submit products and vote/confirm whether
  ingredients are irritating. Crowdsourced flagging
  builds a richer dataset than any one person can
  maintain. Think "Wirecutter for sensitive skin."

  4. Skin profile matcher
  Users input their known sensitivities (e.g., "I react
  to fragrances, shea butter, hyaluronic acid") and the
  app screens products against their specific trigger
  list — not a generic one.

  ---
  Interesting Feature Ideas

  - Camera scan — snap a photo of an ingredient label,
  OCR extracts the text, auto-scan runs
  - INCIDecoder / Open Beauty Facts integration — fetch
  ingredient lists by product name instead of making
  users paste them
  - Reaction journal — log when something stings, itches,
   crusts, etc. (matches the Reason column in your sheet)
  - Ingredient explainer — tap any flagged ingredient to
  see why it's a potential irritant
  - Safe swaps — "this product has 3 bad ingredients,
  here are similar products with 0"
  - Progress dashboard — visualize how your skin
  tolerance or product choices have changed over time

  ---
  Architecture Options

                                                           ---
  The Core Idea                                          
  Your skin profile is the strictest possible baseline —
  if something passes your filter, it's likely safe for
  almost anyone reactive. Users can start from your
  curated list and then personalize from there. You're
  essentially the quality control canary for everyone
  else.

  ---
  How the Ingredient Database Should Work

  Two-tier ingredient system:

  - Canary tier (your defaults) — locked-in entries from
  your existing sheet, clearly marked as "flagged by
  Canary." Users see these and understand why they're
  flagged.
  - Personal tier — each user can add their own known
  irritants or safe ingredients on top. Their additions
  only affect their own profile.

  Updating the master list:
  Since your knowledge evolves, you'd want a simple admin
   flow — you add/edit an ingredient in the Google Sheet
  (or an in-app form), and it propagates as an updated
  default for all users. Users who had already overridden
   an entry would be notified: "The canary flagged
  [ingredient] — update your profile?"

  ---
  Export Thinking

  A few ways to handle this:

  - Export as Google Sheet — generates a new Sheet in the
   user's own Google Drive in the same format as your
  original workbook (3 sheets: ingredients, products,
  data). Feels native, they can keep working in it
  offline.
  - Export as CSV — simpler, universal.
  - Export as PDF report — a clean readable summary of
  their product list with good/bad ingredient highlights.
   More shareable (e.g., to show a dermatologist).

  The Google Sheet export is probably the most useful
  since it mirrors what you already built.

  ---
  User Flow I'd Propose

  Sign in with Google
         ↓
  "Start with Canary defaults?" → Yes / Customize
         ↓
  Product tracker dashboard
    - Add product (paste ingredients or search by name)
    - Auto-scan runs against their ingredient profile
    - Log reactions (Stings / Itches / Crusts / etc.)
         ↓
  Ingredient profile page
    - See all Canary-flagged ingredients
    - Add personal irritants / safe ingredients
    - Override any Canary entry with a note
         ↓
  Export → Google Sheet / CSV / PDF

  ---
  One Key Design Decision

  Who can add products to the shared database vs. just
  their own list?

  Two models:
  - Private only — every user's product list is theirs
  alone. Simpler, more private.
  - Contribute to community — users can optionally share
  a product + their ingredient scan result back to a
  public pool (no personal reaction data, just the
  product). Over time this builds a crowd-sourced product
   database.

  The community model is more powerful long-term but adds
   complexity and moderation. You could start
  private-only and add community contribution later.

  ---
  Tech Stack Recommendation

  Given the Google Sheets integration requirement:

  - Frontend: Next.js (React) — clean, fast, deployable
  on Vercel for free
  - Auth: Google OAuth (via NextAuth) — users sign in
  with Google, you get Drive access automatically
  - Database: Google Sheets API — your master ingredient
  sheet lives in your Drive; user product data lives in
  their own Sheet generated on first login
  - Ingredient updates: You edit your master Sheet → app
  reads it fresh on each scan
  - Export: Google Sheets API to create a new Sheet in
  user's Drive, or generate a CSV/PDF client-side

  This keeps everything in the Google ecosystem, which
  feels right given your existing workflow.

  beginning with the ingredient scanner core (paste →
  scan → results) and layering features from there.

                                                                    Two realistic options:
                                                                    Option A — Search the community database
  Users type a product name, results come from products already in
   your app's database (seeded from your 107 products + community
  additions over time). Fast, no third-party dependency, but only

  Open Beauty Facts is a free, open-source cosmetics database with
   millions of products. Search by name → fetch the ingredient
  list automatically → run the Canary scan. No pasting needed,
  works for almost any product from day one.

  My recommendation: both, in sequence.
  - MVP: Option A only (your seeded database, search + paste
  fallback)
  - V2: Add Open Beauty Facts as a lookup layer so "not found"
  results still work

  This keeps the MVP simple while giving you a clear upgrade path.
   The paste fallback also means a missing product is never a dead
   end — it just requires one extra step.

  So the updated MVP would be:

  ▎ Search by product name → if found, auto-scan → if not found,
  ▎ paste ingredients → scan

  - User pastes a product URL instead of an ingredient list → app

  - iHerb is already linked in your sheet, so that's a natural
  starting point

  Barcode lookup
  - User scans or types a barcode → lookup returns product name +
  ingredients
  - Open Beauty Facts supports this natively; other barcode
  databases (like UPC Item DB) can return product info that links
  to ingredient sources

  AI extraction
  - User pastes any URL (Sephora, brand website, Amazon, wherever)
   → app sends the page to an LLM → LLM extracts the ingredient
  list
  - More flexible than scraping because it handles any site
  without custom parsers
  - Small cost per lookup but very low maintenance

  Camera / OCR
  - User photographs a physical label → Google Cloud Vision or AWS
   Textract reads the text → ingredients extracted
  - Useful for in-store shopping, products not online,
  international brands

● Duplicate merging — keeping user data intact:

  When two products are merged, the deleted duplicate shouldn't     just disappear from anyone's saved list. Here's how to handle
  it:                                                             
  The approach: soft delete + redirect
  - The duplicate is never hard deleted from the database
  - It gets marked as merged_into: [canonical_product_id]
  - Any user who had the duplicate saved automatically sees the
  canonical version instead
  - Their personal notes and reaction logs transfer over to the
  canonical product
  - They'd never notice anything changed

  Admin merge flow:
  Admin flags Product A as duplicate of Product B
  → Review side-by-side (ingredients, source, submission date)
  → Choose which ingredient list is more complete
  → Merge — canonical product keeps best data
  → Duplicate marked merged_into, never deleted
  → All user references silently redirect

  Edge case — conflicting ingredient lists:
  If both versions have different ingredient lists, admin picks
  one or manually combines them before confirming the merge.

  This way no user ever loses a saved product and no data is
  permanently destroyed.

 55 new images saved. Now to your questions about
  the reverse scanner:
                                                       ---
  Product-ingredient junction table                  
  Currently products store ingredients as a text
  string. The junction table approach creates
  explicit relationships:

  CREATE TABLE product_ingredients (
    product_id    uuid REFERENCES products(id) ON
  DELETE CASCADE,
    ingredient_id uuid REFERENCES ingredients(id) ON
  DELETE CASCADE,
    position      integer,
    PRIMARY KEY (product_id, ingredient_id)
  );

  A populate script processes every product's
  ingredient list through the same matching logic as
  the scanner, inserting a row for each recognized
  ingredient. The result: instead of scanning text at
   query time, you query a proper relational table.

  What it enables for the reverse scanner:

  - "Find products with niacinamide and ceramides" →
  WHERE ingredient_id IN (nia_id, cer_id) GROUP BY
  product_id HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT ingredient_id) = 2
  - "Exclude anything with my flagged ingredients" →
  WHERE product_id NOT IN (SELECT product_id FROM
  product_ingredients WHERE ingredient_id IN
  (flagged_ids))
  - Both combined in a single efficient query — no
  text scanning at all

  The main tradeoff: unrecognized ingredients
  (unreviewed) won't appear in the junction table, so
   the exclusion filter only works for classified
  ingredients. A product might have a flagged
  ingredient that's unreviewed — the filter wouldn't
  catch it. You'd want to show a warning like "this
  product has unreviewed ingredients — results may be
   incomplete."

  How to start with approaches 1 and 3:

  I'd build the junction table first (one migration +
   one populate script), then approach 3 ("find
  alternatives" button) is the fastest UI feature on
  top of it — just a single query triggered by a
  button on a flagged result. Approach 1 (ingredient
  picker) is a richer UI that can come after. Want me
   to start with the migration and populate script?
